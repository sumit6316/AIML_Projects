1. What are ADRs?
   Adverse Drug Reactions (ADRs) are harmful or unintended responses
   to a medication taken at normal doses. Example: a patient taking
   aspirin develops internal bleeding.

2. Why is ADR prediction important?
   ADRs cause patient harm, hospitalizations, and even deaths.
   Predicting them early helps doctors take preventive action,
   saving lives and reducing healthcare costs.

3. Why is this a classification problem?
   We are predicting a category (severity level like mild, moderate,
   severe) — not a continuous number. So it's classification.

4. Binary vs Multi-class:
   - Binary: 2 classes (e.g., Severe vs Not Severe)
   - Multi-class: 3+ classes (e.g., Mild / Moderate / Severe)
   Here we treat it as multi-class (3 levels).

5. Why Decision Trees?
   - Easy to understand and visualize
   - Works with both numeric and categorical data
   - No need for feature scaling
   - Shows which features matter most



Feature Selection Justification:
- hospitalized, death: directly indicate severity
- reaction_count: more reactions = more severe
- age, age_group: older patients may react worse
- gender, drug_form, country: demographic/drug factors
- weight: affects drug metabolism


Why F1-Score is useful for imbalanced datasets:
- Accuracy can be misleading when one class dominates.
  Example: If 90% is "Severe", a dumb model that always predicts
  "Severe" gets 90% accuracy but fails for Mild/Moderate.
- F1-Score balances Precision and Recall, giving a fair measure
  across all classes, especially important in medical predictions
  where missing a case (low recall) can be dangerous.

Most Influential Features:
- hospitalized and death: strongest predictors of severity
- reaction_count: more reactions = more severe
- age: older patients face higher risk
- drug_form and weight: moderate influence

Interpretation
  1. Challenges in healthcare data cleaning:
   - Many missing values (patient records are often incomplete)
   - Inconsistent coding of drug names and conditions
   - Imbalanced classes (severe cases are rare)
   - Sensitive data with privacy restrictions

2. Most useful visualization:
   The Correlation Heatmap - it quickly shows which features
   are most related to severity, guiding feature selection.

3. Decision Tree Overfitting:
   Without limits, trees memorize training data perfectly but
   fail on new data (high variance). This is overfitting.

4. Reducing Overfitting:
   - Limit max_depth (e.g., depth=5)
   - Set min_samples_split and min_samples_leaf
   - Use cross-validation to pick best parameters
   - Prune the tree after training

5. Would Random Forest perform better?
   Yes, very likely. Random Forest builds many trees on random
   subsets of data and averages predictions. This reduces
   variance and avoids overfitting — a key weakness of a single
   Decision Tree.
